Homeostasis
Homeostasis is maintained through mechanisms that counteract deviations, primarily
by using negative feedback

Examples of homeostatically maintained variables within the body:
1) body temperature (set point ~37 oC ; regulated by the nervous system)
2) blood glucose (set point ~5 mM; regulated by the endocrine system)
3) blood pH (set point ~7.4; regulated by the respiratory and urinary systems)

Feedback Loops

(cid:1) Negative feedback loops act to reduce deviations of controlled
variables from their set points, restoring them toward equilibrium

(cid:1) Positive feedback loops act to amplify deviations of controlled
variables from their set points, driving them further away from
equilibrium. Examples being childbirth and blood clotting.

Signalling cascade

While signal transduction is the overall pathway, the
cascade represents the step-by-step activation inside
the cell. Features of the cascade:
•One activated molecule activates the next in a
step-by-step manner.

•Each step depends on the previous one.

•Signal flow is in one direction.

•The final activated molecule produces the cellular
response.

•The signal does not directly cause the response —
it is relayed through a sequence of intracellular
proteins, like falling dominos where one triggers
the next.

Signal amplification

Signal amplification:

•A small concentration of ligand produces a large
intracellular response.

•One ligand binds one receptor.

•The receptor activates an amplifier enzyme.

•The amplifier enzyme generates many
intracellular signalling molecules.

•Each step increases the strength of the signal.

•Amplification allows cells to respond strongly to
small concentrations of ligand.
